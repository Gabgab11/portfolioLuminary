Design thoughts · Mar 15, 2023 · 6 min read

Create a landing page that performs great

A landing page has one job: move someone from curious to committed. Everything on it should serve that single job — which means most of what feels tempting to add should be cut.

One offer, one page

The moment a landing page tries to sell two things, it sells neither well. Pick the single action you want — book a table, submit an inquiry, download the menu — and build the whole page around getting someone to that action.

The fold isn't dead, the vague headline is

What kills conversions isn't length, it's ambiguity. A visitor should understand what they're looking at and why it matters to them within the first few seconds, before they've scrolled at all.

Let real specifics do the persuading

"Award-winning cuisine" persuades no one. A real dish, a real photo, a real address, a real review — specifics carry more weight than adjectives ever will.

Make the next step obvious and low-friction

Every extra field in a form, every unclear button label, is a small tax on your conversion rate. Ask for the minimum you need, and say exactly what happens when someone clicks.

The best-performing pages I've built have all shared this: less copy, clearer hierarchy, and a single, unmistakable next step.
